### _A CHILD’S GRAVE AT FLORENCE._

**A.A.E.C.  
Born, July 1848. Died, November 1849**

|   |
|---|
|I.<br><br>Of English blood, of Tuscan birth,<br><br>What country should we give her?<br><br>Instead of any on the earth,<br><br>The civic Heavens receive her.<br><br>II.<br><br>And here among the English tombs<br><br>In Tuscan ground we lay her,<br><br>While the blue Tuscan sky endomes<br><br>Our English words of prayer.<br><br>4<br><br>III.<br><br>A little child!—how long she lived,<br><br>By months, not years, is reckoned:<br><br>Born in one July, she survived<br><br>Alone to see a second.<br><br>IV.<br><br>Bright-featured, as the July sun<br><br>Her little face still played in,<br><br>And splendours, with her birth begun,<br><br>Had had no time for fading.<br><br>V.<br><br>So, Lily, from those July hours,<br><br>No wonder we should call her;<br><br>She looked such kinship to the flowers,—<br><br>Was but a little taller.<br><br>VI.<br><br>A Tuscan Lily,—only white,<br><br>As Dante, in abhorrence<br><br>Of red corruption, wished aright<br><br>The lilies of his Florence.<br><br>5<br><br>VII.<br><br>We could not wish her whiter,—her<br><br>Who perfumed with pure blossom<br><br>The house—a lovely thing to wear<br><br>Upon a mother’s bosom!<br><br>VIII.<br><br>This July creature thought perhaps<br><br>Our speech not worth assuming;<br><br>She sat upon her parents’ laps<br><br>And mimicked the gnat’s humming;<br><br>IX.<br><br>Said “father,” “mother”—then left off,<br><br>For tongues celestial, fitter:<br><br>Her hair had grown just long enough<br><br>To catch heaven’s jasper-glitter.<br><br>X.<br><br>Babes! Love could always hear and see<br><br>Behind the cloud that hid them.<br><br>“Let little children come to Me,<br><br>And do not thou forbid them.”<br><br>6<br><br>XI.<br><br>So, unforbidding, have we met,<br><br>And gently here have laid her,<br><br>Though winter is no time to get<br><br>The flowers that should o’erspread her:<br><br>XII.<br><br>We should bring pansies quick with spring,<br><br>Rose, violet, daffodilly,<br><br>And also, above everything,<br><br>White lilies for our Lily.<br><br>XIII.<br><br>Nay, more than flowers, this grave exacts,—<br><br>Glad, grateful attestations<br><br>Of her sweet eyes and pretty acts,<br><br>With calm renunciations.<br><br>XIV.<br><br>Her very mother with light feet<br><br>Should leave the place too earthy,<br><br>Saying “The angels have thee, Sweet,<br><br>Because we are not worthy.”<br><br>7<br><br>XV.<br><br>But winter kills the orange-buds,<br><br>The gardens in the frost are,<br><br>And all the heart dissolves in floods,<br><br>Remembering we have lost her.<br><br>XVI.<br><br>Poor earth, poor heart,—too weak, too weak<br><br>To miss the July shining!<br><br>Poor heart!—what bitter words we speak<br><br>When God speaks of resigning!<br><br>XVII.<br><br>Sustain this heart in us that faints,<br><br>Thou God, the self-existent!<br><br>We catch up wild at parting saints<br><br>And feel Thy heaven too distant.<br><br>XVIII.<br><br>The wind that swept them out of sin<br><br>Has ruffled all our vesture:<br><br>On the shut door that let them in<br><br>We beat with frantic gesture,—<br><br>8<br><br>XIX.<br><br>To us, us also, open straight!<br><br>The outer life is chilly;<br><br>Are _we_ too, like the earth, to wait<br><br>Till next year for our Lily?<br><br>XX.<br><br>—Oh, my own baby on my knees,<br><br>My leaping, dimpled treasure,<br><br>At every word I write like these,<br><br>Clasped close with stronger pressure!<br><br>XXI.<br><br>Too well my own heart understands,—<br><br>At every word beats fuller—<br><br>My little feet, my little hands,<br><br>And hair of Lily’s colour!<br><br>XXII.<br><br>But God gives patience, Love learns strength,<br><br>And Faith remembers promise,<br><br>And Hope itself can smile at length<br><br>On other hopes gone from us.<br><br>9<br><br>XXIII.<br><br>Love, strong as Death, shall conquer Death,<br><br>Through struggle made more glorious:<br><br>This mother stills her sobbing breath,<br><br>Renouncing yet victorious.<br><br>XXIV.<br><br>Arms, empty of her child, she lifts<br><br>With spirit unbereaven,—<br><br>“God will not all take back His gifts;<br><br>My Lily’s mine in heaven.<br><br>XXV.<br><br>“Still mine! maternal rights serene<br><br>Not given to another!<br><br>The crystal bars shine faint between<br><br>The souls of child and mother.<br><br>XXVI.<br><br>“Meanwhile,” the mother cries, “content!<br><br>Our love was well divided:<br><br>Its sweetness following where she went,<br><br>Its anguish stayed where I did.<br><br>10<br><br>XXVII.<br><br>“Well done of God, to halve the lot,<br><br>And give her all the sweetness;<br><br>To us, the empty room and cot,—<br><br>To her, the Heaven’s completeness.<br><br>XXVIII.<br><br>“To us, this grave,—to her, the rows<br><br>The mystic palm-trees spring in;<br><br>To us, the silence in the house,—<br><br>To her, the choral singing.<br><br>XXIX.<br><br>“For her, to gladden in God’s view,—<br><br>For us, to hope and bear on.<br><br>Grow, Lily, in thy garden new,<br><br>Beside the Rose of Sharon!<br><br>XXX.<br><br>“Grow fast in heaven, sweet Lily clipped,<br><br>In love more calm than this is,<br><br>And may the angels dewy-lipped<br><br>Remind thee of our kisses!<br><br>11<br><br>XXXI.<br><br>“While none shall tell thee of our tears,<br><br>These human tears now falling,<br><br>Till, after a few patient years,<br><br>One home shall take us all in.<br><br>XXXII.<br><br>“Child, father, mother—who, left out?<br><br>Not mother, and not father!<br><br>And when, our dying couch about,<br><br>The natural mists shall gather,<br><br>XXXIII.<br><br>“Some smiling angel close shall stand<br><br>In old Correggio’s fashion,<br><br>And bear a Lily in his hand,<br><br>For death’s ANNUNCIATION.”|

### _CATARINA TO CAMOENS_

#### (DYING IN HIS ABSENCE ABROAD, AND REFERRING TO THE POEM IN WHICH HE RECORDED THE SWEETNESS OF HER EYES).

|   |
|---|
|I.<br><br>On the door you will not enter,<br><br>I have gazed too long: adieu!<br><br>Hope withdraws her peradventure;<br><br>Death is near me,—and not _you_.<br><br>Come, O lover,<br><br>Close and cover<br><br>These poor eyes, you called, I ween,<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen!”<br><br>II.<br><br>When I heard you sing that burden<br><br>In my vernal days and bowers,<br><br>Other praises disregarding,<br><br>I but hearkened that of yours—<br><br>13<br><br>Only saying<br><br>In heart-playing,<br><br>“Blessed eyes mine eyes have been,<br><br>If the sweetest HIS have seen!”<br><br>III.<br><br>But all changes. At this vesper,<br><br>Cold the sun shines down the door.<br><br>If you stood there, would you whisper<br><br>“Love, I love you,” as before,—<br><br>Death pervading<br><br>Now, and shading<br><br>Eyes you sang of, that yestreen,<br><br>As the sweetest ever seen?<br><br>IV.<br><br>Yes. I think, were you beside them,<br><br>Near the bed I die upon,<br><br>Though their beauty you denied them,<br><br>As you stood there, looking down,<br><br>You would truly<br><br>Call them duly,<br><br>For the love’s sake found therein,<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen.”<br><br>14<br><br>V.<br><br>And if _you_ looked down upon them,<br><br>And if _they_ looked up to _you_,<br><br>All the light which has foregone them<br><br>Would be gathered back anew:<br><br>They would truly<br><br>Be as duly<br><br>Love-transformed to beauty’s sheen,<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen.”<br><br>VI.<br><br>But, ah me! you only see me,<br><br>In your thoughts of loving man,<br><br>Smiling soft perhaps and dreamy<br><br>Through the wavings of my fan;<br><br>And unweeting<br><br>Go repeating,<br><br>In your reverie serene,<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen——”<br><br>VII.<br><br>While my spirit leans and reaches<br><br>From my body still and pale,<br><br>Fain to hear what tender speech is<br><br>In your love to help my bale.<br><br>15<br><br>O my poet,<br><br>Come and show it!<br><br>Come, of latest love, to glean<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen.”<br><br>VIII.<br><br>O my poet, O my prophet,<br><br>When you praised their sweetness so,<br><br>Did you think, in singing of it,<br><br>That it might be near to go?<br><br>Had you fancies<br><br>From their glances,<br><br>That the grave would quickly screen<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen”?<br><br>IX.<br><br>No reply. The fountain’s warble<br><br>In the courtyard sounds alone.<br><br>As the water to the marble<br><br>So my heart falls with a moan<br><br>From love-sighing<br><br>To this dying.<br><br>Death forerunneth Love to win<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen.”<br><br>16<br><br>X.<br><br>_Will_ you come? When I’m departed<br><br>Where all sweetnesses are hid,<br><br>Where thy voice, my tender-hearted,<br><br>Will not lift up either lid.<br><br>Cry, O lover,<br><br>Love is over!<br><br>Cry, beneath the cypress green,<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen!”<br><br>XI.<br><br>When the angelus is ringing,<br><br>Near the convent will you walk,<br><br>And recall the choral singing<br><br>Which brought angels down our talk?<br><br>Spirit-shriven<br><br>I viewed Heaven,<br><br>Till you smiled—“Is earth unclean,<br><br>Sweetest eyes were ever seen?”<br><br>XII.<br><br>When beneath the palace-lattice<br><br>You ride slow as you have done,<br><br>And you see a face there that is<br><br>Not the old familiar one,—<br><br>17<br><br>Will you oftly<br><br>Murmur softly,<br><br>“Here ye watched me morn and e’en,<br><br>Sweetest eyes were ever seen!”<br><br>XIII.<br><br>When the palace-ladies, sitting<br><br>Round your gittern, shall have said,<br><br>“Poet, sing those verses written<br><br>For the lady who is dead,”<br><br>Will you tremble<br><br>Yet dissemble,—<br><br>Or sing hoarse, with tears between,<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen”?<br><br>XIV.<br><br>“Sweetest eyes!” how sweet in flowings<br><br>The repeated cadence is!<br><br>Though you sang a hundred poems,<br><br>Still the best one would be this.<br><br>I can hear it<br><br>’Twixt my spirit<br><br>And the earth-noise intervene—<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen!”<br><br>18<br><br>XV.<br><br>But the priest waits for the praying,<br><br>And the choir are on their knees,<br><br>And the soul must pass away in<br><br>Strains more solemn-high than these.<br><br>_Miserere_<br><br>For the weary!<br><br>Oh, no longer for Catrine<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen!”<br><br>XVI.<br><br>Keep my riband, take and keep it,<br><br>(I have loosed it from my hair)[[1]](#Footnote_0001)<br><br>Feeling, while you overweep it,<br><br>Not alone in your despair,<br><br>Since with saintly<br><br>Watch unfaintly<br><br>Out of heaven shall o’er you lean<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen.”<br><br>XVII.<br><br>But—but _now_—yet unremovèd<br><br>Up to heaven, they glisten fast;<br><br>19<br><br>You may cast away, Belovèd,<br><br>In your future all my past:<br><br>Such old phrases<br><br>May be praises<br><br>For some fairer bosom-queen—<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen!”<br><br>XVIII.<br><br>Eyes of mine, what are ye doing?<br><br>Faithless, faithless,—praised amiss<br><br>If a tear be of your showing,<br><br>Dropt for any hope of HIS!<br><br>Death has boldness<br><br>Besides coldness,<br><br>If unworthy tears demean<br><br>“Sweetest eyes were ever seen.”<br><br>XIX.<br><br>I will look out to his future;<br><br>I will bless it till it shine.<br><br>Should he ever be a suitor<br><br>Unto sweeter eyes than mine,<br><br>Sunshine gild them,<br><br>Angels shield them,<br><br>Whatsoever eyes terrene<br><br>_Be_ the sweetest HIS have seen!|

### _LIFE AND LOVE._

|   |
|---|
|I.<br><br>Fast this Life of mine was dying,<br><br>Blind already and calm as death,<br><br>Snowflakes on her bosom lying<br><br>Scarcely heaving with her breath.<br><br>II.<br><br>Love came by, and having known her<br><br>In a dream of fabled lands,<br><br>Gently stooped, and laid upon her<br><br>Mystic chrism of holy hands;<br><br>III.<br><br>Drew his smile across her folded<br><br>Eyelids, as the swallow dips;<br><br>Breathed as finely as the cold did<br><br>Through the locking of her lips.<br><br>21<br><br>IV.<br><br>So, when Life looked upward, being<br><br>Warmed and breathed on from above,<br><br>What sight could she have for seeing,<br><br>Evermore ... but only Love?|

### _A DENIAL._

|   |
|---|
|I.<br><br>We have met late—it is too late to meet,<br><br>O friend, not more than friend!<br><br>Death’s forecome shroud is tangled round my feet,<br><br>And if I step or stir, I touch the end.<br><br>In this last jeopardy<br><br>Can I approach thee, I, who cannot move?<br><br>How shall I answer thy request for love?<br><br>Look in my face and see.<br><br>II.<br><br>I love thee not, I dare not love thee! go<br><br>In silence; drop my hand.<br><br>If thou seek roses, seek them where they blow<br><br>In garden-alleys, not in desert-sand.<br><br>Can life and death agree,<br><br>That thou shouldst stoop thy song to my complaint?<br><br>I cannot love thee. If the word is faint,<br><br>Look in my face and see.<br><br>23<br><br>III.<br><br>I might have loved thee in some former days.<br><br>Oh, then, my spirits had leapt<br><br>As now they sink, at hearing thy love-praise!<br><br>Before these faded cheeks were overwept,<br><br>Had this been asked of me,<br><br>To love thee with my whole strong heart and head,—<br><br>I should have said still ... yes, but _smiled_ and said,<br><br>“Look in my face and see!”<br><br>IV.<br><br>But now ... God sees me, God, who took my heart<br><br>And drowned it in life’s surge.<br><br>In all your wide warm earth I have no part—<br><br>A light song overcomes me like a dirge.<br><br>Could Love’s great harmony<br><br>The saints keep step to when their bonds are loose,<br><br>Not weigh me down? am _I_ a wife to choose?<br><br>Look in my face and see—<br><br>V.<br><br>While I behold, as plain as one who dreams,<br><br>Some woman of full worth,<br><br>Whose voice, as cadenced as a silver stream’s,<br><br>Shall prove the fountain-soul which sends it forth;<br><br>24<br><br>One younger, more thought-free<br><br>And fair and gay, than I, thou must forget,<br><br>With brighter eyes than these ... which are not wet ...<br><br>Look in my face and see!<br><br>VI.<br><br>So farewell thou, whom I have known too late<br><br>To let thee come so near.<br><br>Be counted happy while men call thee great,<br><br>And one belovèd woman feels thee dear!—<br><br>Not I!—that cannot be.<br><br>I am lost, I am changed,—I must go farther, where<br><br>The change shall take me worse, and no one dare<br><br>Look in my face and see.<br><br>VII.<br><br>Meantime I bless thee. By these thoughts of mine<br><br>I bless thee from all such!<br><br>I bless thy lamp to oil, thy cup to wine,<br><br>Thy hearth to joy, thy hand to an equal touch<br><br>Of loyal troth. For me,<br><br>I love thee not, I love thee not!—away!<br><br>Here’s no more courage in my soul to say<br><br>“Look in my face and see.”|

### _PROOF AND DISPROOF._

|   |
|---|
|I.<br><br>Dost thou love me, my Belovèd?<br><br>Who shall answer yes or no?<br><br>What is provèd or disprovèd<br><br>When my soul inquireth so,<br><br>Dost thou love me, my Belovèd?<br><br>II.<br><br>I have seen thy heart to-day,<br><br>Never open to the crowd,<br><br>While to love me aye and aye<br><br>Was the vow as it was vowed<br><br>By thine eyes of steadfast grey.<br><br>26<br><br>III.<br><br>Now I sit alone, alone—<br><br>And the hot tears break and burn,<br><br>Now, Belovèd, thou art gone,<br><br>Doubt and terror have their turn.<br><br>_Is_ it love that I have known?<br><br>IV.<br><br>I have known some bitter things,—<br><br>Anguish, anger, solitude.<br><br>Year by year an evil brings,<br><br>Year by year denies a good;<br><br>March winds violate my springs.<br><br>V.<br><br>I have known how sickness bends,<br><br>I have known how sorrow breaks,—<br><br>How quick hopes have sudden ends,<br><br>How the heart thinks till it aches<br><br>Of the smile of buried friends.<br><br>27<br><br>VI.<br><br>Last, I have known _thee_, my brave<br><br>Noble thinker, lover, doer!<br><br>The best knowledge last I have.<br><br>But thou comest as the thrower<br><br>Of fresh flowers upon a grave.<br><br>VII.<br><br>Count what feelings used to move me!<br><br>Can this love assort with those?<br><br>Thou, who art so far above me,<br><br>Wilt thou stoop so, for repose?<br><br>Is it true that thou canst love me?<br><br>VIII.<br><br>Do not blame me if I doubt thee.<br><br>I can call love by its name<br><br>When thine arm is wrapt about me;<br><br>But even love seems not the same,<br><br>When I sit alone, without thee.<br><br>28<br><br>IX.<br><br>In thy clear eyes I descried<br><br>Many a proof of love, to-day;<br><br>But to-night, those unbelied<br><br>Speechful eyes being gone away,<br><br>There’s the proof to seek, beside.<br><br>X.<br><br>Dost thou love me, my Belovèd?<br><br>Only _thou_ canst answer yes!<br><br>And, thou gone, the proof’s disprovèd,<br><br>And the cry rings answerless—<br><br>Dost thou love me, my Belovèd?|

### _QUESTION AND ANSWER._

|   |
|---|
|I.<br><br>Love you seek for, presupposes<br><br>Summer heat and sunny glow.<br><br>Tell me, do you find moss-roses<br><br>Budding, blooming in the snow?<br><br>Snow might kill the rose-tree’s root—<br><br>Shake it quickly from your foot,<br><br>Lest it harm you as you go.<br><br>II.<br><br>From the ivy where it dapples<br><br>A grey ruin, stone by stone,<br><br>Do you look for grapes or apples,<br><br>Or for sad green leaves alone?<br><br>Pluck the leaves off, two or three—<br><br>Keep them for morality<br><br>When you shall be safe and gone.|

### _INCLUSIONS._

|   |
|---|
|I.<br><br>Oh, wilt thou have my hand, Dear, to lie along in thine?<br><br>As a little stone in a running stream, it seems to lie and pine.<br><br>Now drop the poor pale hand, Dear, unfit to plight with thine.<br><br>II.<br><br>Oh, wilt thou have my cheek, Dear, drawn closer to thine own?<br><br>My cheek is white, my cheek is worn, by many a tear run down.<br><br>Now leave a little space, Dear, lest it should wet thine own.<br><br>31<br><br>III.<br><br>Oh, must thou have my soul, Dear, commingled with thy soul?—<br><br>Red grows the cheek, and warm the hand; the part is in the whole:<br><br>Nor hands nor cheeks keep separate, when soul is joined to soul.|

### _INSUFFICIENCY._

|   |
|---|
|I.<br><br>There is no one beside thee and no one above thee,<br><br>Thou standest alone as the nightingale sings!<br><br>And my words that would praise thee are impotent things,<br><br>For none can express thee though all should approve thee.<br><br>I love thee so, Dear, that I only can love thee.<br><br>II.<br><br>Say, what can I do for thee? weary thee, grieve thee?<br><br>Lean on thy shoulder, new burdens to add?<br><br>Weep my tears over thee, making thee sad?<br><br>Oh, hold me not—love me not! let me retrieve thee.<br><br>I love thee so, Dear, that I only can leave thee.|

|   |
|---|
|I thought once how Theocritus had sung<br><br>Of the sweet years, the dear and wished-for years,<br><br>Who each one in a gracious hand appears<br><br>To bear a gift for mortals, old or young:<br><br>And, as I mused it in his antique tongue,<br><br>I saw, in gradual vision through my tears,<br><br>The sweet, sad years, the melancholy years,<br><br>Those of my own life, who by turns had flung<br><br>A shadow across me. Straightway I was ’ware,<br><br>So weeping, how a mystic Shape did move<br><br>Behind me, and drew me backward by the hair;<br><br>And a voice said in mastery, while I strove,—<br><br>“Guess now who holds thee?”—“Death,” I said. But, there,<br><br>The silver answer rang,—“Not Death, but Love.”|

---

36

### II.

|   |
|---|
|But only three in all God’s universe<br><br>Have heard this word thou hast said,—Himself, beside<br><br>Thee speaking, and me listening! and replied<br><br>One of us ... _that_ was God, ... and laid the curse<br><br>So darkly on my eyelids, as to amerce<br><br>My sight from seeing thee,—that if I had died,<br><br>The deathweights, placed there, would have signified<br><br>Less absolute exclusion. “Nay” is worse<br><br>From God than from all others, O my friend!<br><br>Men could not part us with their worldly jars,<br><br>Nor the seas change us, nor the tempests bend;<br><br>Our hands would touch for all the mountain-bars:<br><br>And, heaven being rolled between us at the end,<br><br>We should but vow the faster for the stars.|

---

37

### III.

|   |
|---|
|Unlike are we, unlike, O princely Heart!<br><br>Unlike our uses and our destinies.<br><br>Our ministering two angels look surprise<br><br>On one another, as they strike athwart<br><br>Their wings in passing. Thou, bethink thee, art<br><br>A guest for queens to social pageantries,<br><br>With gages from a hundred brighter eyes<br><br>Than tears even can make mine, to play thy part<br><br>Of chief musician. What hast _thou_ to do<br><br>With looking from the lattice-lights at me,<br><br>A poor, tired, wandering singer, singing through<br><br>The dark, and leaning up a cypress tree?<br><br>The chrism is on thine head,—on mine, the dew,—<br><br>And Death must dig the level where these agree.|

---

38

### IV.

|   |
|---|
|Thou hast thy calling to some palace-floor,<br><br>Most gracious singer of high poems! where<br><br>The dancers will break footing, from the care<br><br>Of watching up thy pregnant lips for more.<br><br>And dost thou lift this house’s latch too poor<br><br>For hand of thine? and canst thou think and bear<br><br>To let thy music drop here unaware<br><br>In folds of golden fulness at my door?<br><br>Look up and see the casement broken in,<br><br>The bats and owlets builders in the roof!<br><br>My cricket chirps against thy mandolin.<br><br>Hush, call no echo up in further proof<br><br>Of desolation! there’s a voice within<br><br>That weeps ... as thou must sing ... alone, aloof.|

---

39

### V.

|   |
|---|
|I lift my heavy heart up solemnly,<br><br>As once Electra her sepulchral urn,<br><br>And, looking in thine eyes, I overturn<br><br>The ashes at thy feet. Behold and see<br><br>What a great heap of grief lay hid in me,<br><br>And how the red wild sparkles dimly burn<br><br>Through the ashen greyness. If thy foot in scorn<br><br>Could tread them out to darkness utterly,<br><br>It might be well perhaps. But if instead<br><br>Thou wait beside me for the wind to blow<br><br>The grey dust up, ... those laurels on thine head,<br><br>O my Belovèd, will not shield thee so,<br><br>That none of all the fires shall scorch and shred<br><br>The hair beneath. Stand further off then! go.|

---

40

### VI.

|   |
|---|
|Go from me. Yet I feel that I shall stand<br><br>Henceforward in thy shadow. Nevermore<br><br>Alone upon the threshold of my door<br><br>Of individual life, I shall command<br><br>The uses of my soul, nor lift my hand<br><br>Serenely in the sunshine as before,<br><br>Without the sense of that which I forbore—<br><br>Thy touch upon the palm. The widest land<br><br>Doom takes to part us, leaves thy heart in mine<br><br>With pulses that beat double. What I do<br><br>And what I dream include thee, as the wine<br><br>Must taste of its own grapes. And when I sue<br><br>God for myself, He hears that name of thine,<br><br>And sees within my eyes the tears of two.|

---

41

### VII.

|   |
|---|
|The face of all the world is changed, I think,<br><br>Since first I heard the footsteps of thy soul<br><br>Move still, oh, still, beside me, as they stole<br><br>Betwixt me and the dreadful outer brink<br><br>Of obvious death, where I, who thought to sink,<br><br>Was caught up into love, and taught the whole<br><br>Of life in a new rhythm. The cup of dole<br><br>God gave for baptism, I am fain to drink,<br><br>And praise its sweetness, Sweet, with thee anear.<br><br>The names of country, heaven, are changed away<br><br>For where thou art or shalt be, there or here;<br><br>And this ... this lute and song ... loved yesterday,<br><br>(The singing angels know) are only dear<br><br>Because thy name moves right in what they say.|

---

42

### VIII.

|   |
|---|
|What can I give thee back, O liberal<br><br>And princely giver, who hast brought the gold<br><br>And purple of thine heart, unstained, untold,<br><br>And laid them on the outside of the wall<br><br>For such as I to take or leave withal,<br><br>In unexpected largesse? am I cold,<br><br>Ungrateful, that for these most manifold<br><br>High gifts, I render nothing back at all?<br><br>Not so; not cold,—but very poor instead<br><br>Ask God who knows. For frequent tears have run<br><br>The colours from my life, and left so dead<br><br>And pale a stuff, it were not fitly done<br><br>To give the same as pillow to thy head.<br><br>Go farther! let it serve to trample on.|

---

43

### IX.

|   |
|---|
|Can it be right to give what I can give?<br><br>To let thee sit beneath the fall of tears<br><br>As salt as mine, and hear the sighing years<br><br>Re-sighing on my lips renunciative<br><br>Through those infrequent smiles which fail to live<br><br>For all thy adjurations? O my fears,<br><br>That this can scarce be right! We are not peers,<br><br>So to be lovers; and I own, and grieve,<br><br>That givers of such gifts as mine are, must<br><br>Be counted with the ungenerous. Out, alas!<br><br>I will not soil thy purple with my dust,<br><br>Nor breathe my poison on thy Venice-glass,<br><br>Nor give thee any love—which were unjust.<br><br>Beloved, I only love thee! let it pass.|

---

44

### X.

|   |
|---|
|Yet, love, mere love, is beautiful indeed<br><br>And worthy of acceptation. Fire is bright,<br><br>Let temple burn, or flax; an equal light<br><br>Leaps in the flame from cedar-plank or weed:<br><br>And love is fire. And when I say at need<br><br>_I love thee_ ... mark!... _I love thee_—in thy sight<br><br>I stand transfigured, glorified aright,<br><br>With conscience of the new rays that proceed<br><br>Out of my face toward thine. There’s nothing low<br><br>In love, when love the lowest: meanest creatures<br><br>Who love God, God accepts while loving so.<br><br>And what I _feel_, across the inferior features<br><br>Of what I _am_, doth flash itself, and show<br><br>How that great work of Love enhances Nature’s.|

---

45

### XI.

|   |
|---|
|And therefore if to love can be desert,<br><br>I am not all unworthy. Cheeks as pale<br><br>As these you see, and trembling knees that fail<br><br>To bear the burden of a heavy heart,—<br><br>This weary minstrel-life that once was girt<br><br>To climb Aornus, and can scarce avail<br><br>To pipe now ’gainst the valley nightingale<br><br>A melancholy music,—why advert<br><br>To these things? O Belovèd, it is plain<br><br>I am not of thy worth nor for thy place!<br><br>And yet, because I love thee, I obtain<br><br>From that same love this vindicating grace,<br><br>To live on still in love, and yet in vain,—<br><br>To bless thee, yet renounce thee to thy face.|

---

46

### XII.

|   |
|---|
|Indeed this very love which is my boast,<br><br>And which, when rising up from breast to brow,<br><br>Doth crown me with a ruby large enow<br><br>To draw men’s eyes and prove the inner cost,—<br><br>This love even, all my worth, to the uttermost,<br><br>I should not love withal, unless that thou<br><br>Hadst set me an example, shown me how,<br><br>When first thine earnest eyes with mine were crossed,<br><br>And love called love. And thus, I cannot speak<br><br>Of love even, as a good thing of my own:<br><br>Thy soul hath snatched up mine all faint and weak,<br><br>And placed it by thee on a golden throne,—<br><br>And that I love (O soul, we must be meek!)<br><br>Is by thee only, whom I love alone.|

---

47

### XIII.

|   |
|---|
|And wilt thou have me fashion into speech<br><br>The love I bear thee, finding words enough,<br><br>And hold the torch out, while the winds are rough,<br><br>Between our faces, to cast light on each?—<br><br>I drop it at thy feet. I cannot teach<br><br>My hand to hold my spirit so far off<br><br>From myself—me—that I should bring thee proof<br><br>In words, of love hid in me out of reach.<br><br>Nay, let the silence of my womanhood<br><br>Commend my woman-love to thy belief,—<br><br>Seeing that I stand unwon, however wooed,<br><br>And rend the garment of my life, in brief,<br><br>By a most dauntless, voiceless fortitude,<br><br>Lest one touch of this heart convey its grief.|

---

48

### XIV.

|   |
|---|
|If thou must love me, let it be for nought<br><br>Except for love’s sake only. Do not say<br><br>“I love her for her smile—her look—her way<br><br>Of speaking gently,—for a trick of thought<br><br>That falls in well with mine, and certes brought<br><br>A sense of pleasant ease on such a day”—<br><br>For these things in themselves, Belovèd, may<br><br>Be changed, or change for thee,—and love, so wrought,<br><br>May be unwrought so. Neither love me for<br><br>Thine own dear pity’s wiping my cheeks dry,—<br><br>A creature might forget to weep, who bore<br><br>Thy comfort long, and lose thy love thereby!<br><br>But love me for love’s sake, that evermore<br><br>Thou mayst love on, through love’s eternity.|

---

49

### XV.

|   |
|---|
|Accuse me not, beseech thee, that I wear<br><br>Too calm and sad a face in front of thine;<br><br>For we two look two ways, and cannot shine<br><br>With the same sunlight on our brow and hair.<br><br>On me thou lookest with no doubting care,<br><br>As on a bee shut in a crystalline;<br><br>Since sorrow hath shut me safe in love’s divine,<br><br>And to spread wing and fly in the outer air<br><br>Were most impossible failure, if I strove<br><br>To fail so. But I look on thee—on thee—<br><br>Beholding, besides love, the end of love,<br><br>Hearing oblivion beyond memory;<br><br>As one who sits and gazes from above,<br><br>Over the rivers to the bitter sea.|

---

50

### XVI.

|   |
|---|
|And yet, because thou overcomest so,<br><br>Because thou art more noble and like a king,<br><br>Thou canst prevail against my fears and fling<br><br>Thy purple round me, till my heart shall grow<br><br>Too close against thine heart henceforth to know<br><br>How it shook when alone. Why, conquering<br><br>May prove as lordly and complete a thing<br><br>In lifting upward, as in crushing low!<br><br>And as a vanquished soldier yields his sword<br><br>To one who lifts him from the bloody earth,<br><br>Even so, Belovèd, I at last record,<br><br>Here ends my strife. If _thou_ invite me forth,<br><br>I rise above abasement at the word.<br><br>Make thy love larger to enlarge my worth.|

---

51

### XVII.

|   |
|---|
|My poet, thou canst touch on all the notes<br><br>God set between His After and Before,<br><br>And strike up and strike off the general roar<br><br>Of the rushing worlds a melody that floats<br><br>In a serene air purely. Antidotes<br><br>Of medicated music, answering for<br><br>Mankind’s forlornest uses, thou canst pour<br><br>From thence into their ears. God’s will devotes<br><br>Thine to such ends, and mine to wait on thine.<br><br>How, Dearest, wilt thou have me for most use?<br><br>A hope, to sing by gladly? or a fine<br><br>Sad memory, with thy songs to interfuse?<br><br>A shade, in which to sing—of palm or pine?<br><br>A grave, on which to rest from singing? Choose.|

---

52

### XVIII.

|   |
|---|
|I never gave a lock of hair away<br><br>To a man, Dearest, except this to thee,<br><br>Which now upon my fingers thoughtfully,<br><br>I ring out to the full brown length and say<br><br>“Take it.” My day of youth went yesterday;<br><br>My hair no longer bounds to my foot’s glee,<br><br>Nor plant I it from rose or myrtle-tree,<br><br>As girls do, any more: it only may<br><br>Now shade on two pale cheeks the mark of tears,<br><br>Taught drooping from the head that hangs aside<br><br>Through sorrow’s trick. I thought the funeral-shears<br><br>Would take this first, but Love is justified,—<br><br>Take it thou,—finding pure, from all those years,<br><br>The kiss my mother left here when she died.|

---

53

### XIX.

|   |
|---|
|The soul’s Rialto hath its merchandise;<br><br>I barter curl for curl upon that mart,<br><br>And from my poet’s forehead to my heart<br><br>Receive this lock which outweighs argosies,—<br><br>As purply black, as erst to Pindar’s eyes<br><br>The dim purpureal tresses gloomed athwart<br><br>The nine white Muse-brows. For this counterpart, ...<br><br>The bay-crown’s shade, Belovèd, I surmise,<br><br>Still lingers on thy curl, it is so black!<br><br>Thus, with a fillet of smooth-kissing breath,<br><br>I tie the shadows safe from gliding back,<br><br>And lay the gift where nothing hindereth;<br><br>Here on my heart, as on thy brow, to lack<br><br>No natural heat till mine grows cold in death.|

---

54

### XX.

|   |
|---|
|Beloved, my Belovèd, when I think<br><br>That thou wast in the world a year ago,<br><br>What time I sat alone here in the snow<br><br>And saw no footprint, heard the silence sink<br><br>No moment at thy voice, but, link by link,<br><br>Went counting all my chains as if that so<br><br>They never could fall off at any blow<br><br>Struck by thy possible hand,—why, thus I drink<br><br>Of life’s great cup of wonder! Wonderful,<br><br>Never to feel thee thrill the day or night<br><br>With personal act or speech,—nor ever cull<br><br>Some prescience of thee with the blossoms white<br><br>Thou sawest growing! Atheists are as dull,<br><br>Who cannot guess God’s presence out of sight.|

---

55

### XXI.

|   |
|---|
|Say over again, and yet once over again,<br><br>That thou dost love me. Though the word repeated<br><br>Should seem “a cuckoo-song,” as thou dost treat it.<br><br>Remember, never to the hill or plain,<br><br>Valley and wood, without her cuckoo-strain<br><br>Comes the fresh Spring in all her green completed.<br><br>Belovèd, I, amid the darkness greeted<br><br>By a doubtful spirit-voice, in that doubt’s pain<br><br>Cry, “Speak once more—thou lovest!” Who can fear<br><br>Too many stars, though each in heaven shall roll,<br><br>Too many flowers, though each shall crown the year?<br><br>Say thou dost love me, love me, love me—toll<br><br>The silver iterance!—only minding, Dear,<br><br>To love me also in silence with thy soul.|

---

56

### XXII.

|   |
|---|
|When our two souls stand up erect and strong,<br><br>Face to face, silent, drawing nigh and nigher,<br><br>Until the lengthening wings break into fire<br><br>At either curvèd point,—what bitter wrong<br><br>Can the earth do to us, that we should not long<br><br>Be here contented? Think. In mounting higher,<br><br>The angels would press on us and aspire<br><br>To drop some golden orb of perfect song<br><br>Into our deep, dear silence. Let us stay<br><br>Rather on earth, Belovèd,—where the unfit<br><br>Contrarious moods of men recoil away<br><br>And isolate pure spirits, and permit<br><br>A place to stand and love in for a day,<br><br>With darkness and the death-hour rounding it.|

---

57

### XXIII.

|   |
|---|
|Is it indeed so? If I lay here dead,<br><br>Wouldst thou miss any life in losing mine?<br><br>And would the sun for thee more coldly shine<br><br>Because of grave-damps falling round my head?<br><br>I marvelled, my Belovèd, when I read<br><br>Thy thought so in the letter. I am thine—<br><br>But ... _so_ much to thee? Can I pour thy wine<br><br>While my hands tremble? Then my soul, instead<br><br>Of dreams of death, resumes life’s lower range.<br><br>Then, love me, Love! look on me—breathe on me!<br><br>As brighter ladies do not count it strange,<br><br>For love, to give up acres and degree,<br><br>I yield the grave for thy sake, and exchange<br><br>My near sweet view of Heaven, for earth with thee!|

---

58

### XXIV.

|   |
|---|
|Let the world’s sharpness, like a clasping knife,<br><br>Shut in upon itself and do no harm<br><br>In this close hand of Love, now soft and warm,<br><br>And let us hear no sound of human strife<br><br>After the click of the shutting. Life to life—<br><br>I lean upon thee, Dear, without alarm,<br><br>And feel as safe as guarded by a charm<br><br>Against the stab of worldlings, who if rife<br><br>Are weak to injure. Very-whitely still<br><br>The lilies of our lives may reassure<br><br>Their blossoms from their roots, accessible<br><br>Alone to heavenly dews that drop not fewer<br><br>Growing straight, out of man’s reach, on the hill.<br><br>God only, who made us rich, can make us poor.|

---

59

### XXV.

|   |
|---|
|A heavy heart, Belovèd, have I borne<br><br>From year to year until I saw thy face,<br><br>And sorrow after sorrow took the place<br><br>Of all those natural joys as lightly worn<br><br>As the stringed pearls, each lifted in its turn<br><br>By a beating heart at dance-time. Hopes apace<br><br>Were changed to long despairs, till God’s own grace<br><br>Could scarcely lift above the world forlorn<br><br>My heavy heart. Then _thou_ didst bid me bring<br><br>And let it drop adown thy calmly great<br><br>Deep being! Fast it sinketh, as a thing<br><br>Which its own nature doth precipitate,<br><br>While thine doth close above it, mediating<br><br>Betwixt the stars and the unaccomplished fate.|

---

60

### XXVI.

|   |
|---|
|I lived with visions for my company<br><br>Instead of men and women, years ago,<br><br>And found them gentle mates, nor thought to know<br><br>A sweeter music than they played to me.<br><br>But soon their trailing purple was not free<br><br>Of this world’s dust, their lutes did silent grow,<br><br>And I myself grew faint and blind below<br><br>Their vanishing eyes. Then THOU didst come—to be,<br><br>Belovèd, what they seemed. Their shining fronts,<br><br>Their songs, their splendours (better, yet the same,<br><br>As river-water hallowed into fonts),<br><br>Met in thee, and from out thee overcame<br><br>My soul with satisfaction of all wants:<br><br>Because God’s gifts put man’s best dreams to shame.|

---

61

### XXVII.

|   |
|---|
|My own Belovèd, who hast lifted me<br><br>From this drear flat of earth where I was thrown,<br><br>And, in betwixt the languid ringlets, blown<br><br>A life-breath, till the forehead hopefully<br><br>Shines out again, as all the angels see,<br><br>Before thy saving kiss! My own, my own,<br><br>Who camest to me when the world was gone,<br><br>And I who looked for only God, found _thee_!<br><br>I find thee; I am safe, and strong, and glad.<br><br>As one who stands in dewless asphodel<br><br>Looks backward on the tedious time he had<br><br>In the upper life,—so I, with bosom-swell,<br><br>Make witness, here, between the good and bad,<br><br>That Love, as strong as Death, retrieves as well.|

---

62

### XXVIII.

|   |
|---|
|My letters! all dead paper, mute and white!<br><br>And yet they seem alive and quivering<br><br>Against my tremulous hands which loose the string<br><br>And let them drop down on my knee to-night.<br><br>This said,—he wished to have me in his sight<br><br>Once, as a friend: this fixed a day in spring<br><br>To come and touch my hand ... a simple thing,<br><br>Yet I wept for it!—this, ... the paper’s light ...<br><br>Said, _Dear, I love thee_; and I sank and quailed<br><br>As if God’s future thundered on my past.<br><br>This said, _I am thine_—and so its ink has paled<br><br>With lying at my heart that beat too fast.<br><br>And this ... O Love, thy words have ill availed<br><br>If, what this said, I dared repeat at last!|

---

63

### XXIX.

|   |
|---|
|I think of thee!—my thoughts do twine and bud<br><br>About thee, as wild vines, about a tree,<br><br>Put out broad leaves, and soon there’s nought to see<br><br>Except the straggling green which hides the wood.<br><br>Yet, O my palm-tree, be it understood<br><br>I will not have my thoughts instead of thee<br><br>Who art dearer, better! Rather, instantly<br><br>Renew thy presence; as a strong tree should,<br><br>Rustle thy boughs and set thy trunk all bare,<br><br>And let these bands of greenery which insphere thee<br><br>Drop heavily down,—burst, shattered, everywhere!<br><br>Because, in this deep joy to see and hear thee<br><br>And breathe within thy shadow a new air,<br><br>I do not think of thee—I am too near thee.|

---

64

### XXX.

|   |
|---|
|I see thine image through my tears to-night,<br><br>And yet to-day I saw thee smiling. How<br><br>Refer the cause?—Belovèd, is it thou<br><br>Or I, who makes me sad? The acolyte<br><br>Amid the chanted joy and thankful rite<br><br>May so fall flat, with pale insensate brow,<br><br>On the altar-stair. I hear thy voice and vow,<br><br>Perplexed, uncertain, since thou art out of sight,<br><br>As he, in his swooning ears, the choir’s Amen.<br><br>Belovèd, dost thou love? or did I see all<br><br>The glory as I dreamed, and fainted when<br><br>Too vehement light dilated my ideal,<br><br>For my soul’s eyes? Will that light come again,<br><br>As now these tears come—falling hot and real?|

---

65

### XXXI.

|   |
|---|
|Thou comest! all is said without a word.<br><br>I sit beneath thy looks, as children do<br><br>In the noon-sun, with souls that tremble through<br><br>Their happy eyelids from an unaverred<br><br>Yet prodigal inward joy. Behold, I erred<br><br>In that last doubt! and yet I cannot rue<br><br>The sin most, but the occasion—that we two<br><br>Should for a moment stand unministered<br><br>By a mutual presence. Ah, keep near and close,<br><br>Thou dovelike help! and, when my fears would rise,<br><br>With thy broad heart serenely interpose:<br><br>Brood down with thy divine sufficiencies<br><br>These thoughts which tremble when bereft of those,<br><br>Like callow birds left desert to the skies.|

---

66

### XXXII.

|   |
|---|
|The first time that the sun rose on thine oath<br><br>To love me, I looked forward to the moon<br><br>To slacken all those bonds which seemed too soon<br><br>And quickly tied to make a lasting troth.<br><br>Quick-loving hearts, I thought, may quickly loathe;<br><br>And, looking on myself, I seemed not one<br><br>For such man’s love!—more like an out-of-tune<br><br>Worn viol, a good singer would be wroth<br><br>To spoil his song with, and which, snatched in haste,<br><br>Is laid down at the first ill-sounding note.<br><br>I did not wrong myself so, but I placed<br><br>A wrong on _thee_. For perfect strains may float<br><br>’Neath master-hands, from instruments defaced,—<br><br>And great souls, at one stroke, may do and doat.|

---

67

### XXXIII.

|   |
|---|
|Yes, call me by my pet-name! let me hear<br><br>The name I used to run at, when a child,<br><br>From innocent play, and leave the cowslips piled,<br><br>To glance up in some face that proved me dear<br><br>With the look of its eyes. I miss the clear<br><br>Fond voices which, being drawn and reconciled<br><br>Into the music of Heaven’s undefiled,<br><br>Call me no longer. Silence on the bier,<br><br>While I call God—call God!—So let thy mouth<br><br>Be heir to those who are now exanimate.<br><br>Gather the north flowers to complete the south,<br><br>And catch the early love up in the late.<br><br>Yes, call me by that name,—and I, in truth,<br><br>With the same heart, will answer and not wait.|

---

68

### XXXIV.

## CASA GUIDI WINDOWS

### A Poem,  
IN TWO PARTS

81

### ADVERTISEMENT TO THE FIRST EDITION.

This poem contains the impressions of the writer upon events in Tuscany of which she was a witness. “From a window,” the critic may demur. She bows to the objection in the very title of her work. No continuous narrative nor exposition of political philosophy is attempted by her. It is a simple story of personal impressions, whose only value is in the intensity with which they were received, as proving her warm affection for a beautiful and unfortunate country, and the sincerity with which they are related, as indicating her own good faith and freedom from partisanship.

Of the two parts of this poem, the first was written nearly three years ago, while the second resumes the actual situation of 1851. The discrepancy between the two parts is a sufficient guarantee to the public of the truthfulness of the writer, who, though she certainly escaped the epidemic “falling sickness” of enthusiasm for Pio Nono, takes shame upon herself that she believed, like a woman, some royal oaths, and lost sight of the probable consequences of some obvious popular defects. If the discrepancy should be painful to the reader, let him understand that to the writer it has been more so. But such discrepancies we are called upon to accept at every hour by the conditions of our nature, 82 implying the interval between aspiration and performance, between faith and disillusion, between hope and fact.

|   |

|“O trusted broken prophecy,<br><br>O richest fortune sourly crost,<br><br>Born for the future, to the future lost!”|

Nay, not lost to the future in this case. The future of Italy shall not be disinherited.

Florence, 1851.

### PREFACE.

These poems were written under the pressure of the events they indicate, after a residence in Italy of so many years that the present triumph of great principles is heightened to the writer’s feelings by the disastrous issue of the last movement, witnessed from “Casa Guidi Windows” in 1849. Yet, if the verses should appear to English readers too pungently rendered to admit of a patriotic respect to the English sense of things, I will not excuse myself on such grounds, nor on the ground of my attachment to the Italian people and my admiration of their heroic constancy and union. What I have written has simply been written because I love truth and justice _quand même_,—“more than Plato” and Plato’s country, more than Dante and Dante’s country, more even than Shakespeare and Shakespeare’s country.

And if patriotism means the flattery of one’s nation in every case, then the patriot, take it as you please, is merely the courtier which I am not, though I have written “Napoleon III. in Italy.” It is time to limit the significance of certain terms, or to enlarge the significance of certain things. Nationality is excellent in its place; and the instinct of self-love is the root of a man, which will develop into sacrificial virtues. But all the virtues are means and uses; and, if we hinder their tendency to growth and expansion, we both destroy them as virtues, and degrade them to that rankest species of corruption reserved for the most noble organizations. For instance,—non-intervention in the affairs of neighbouring states is a high political virtue; but non-intervention 170 does not mean, passing by on the other side when your neighbour falls among thieves,—or Phariseeism would recover it from Christianity. Freedom itself is virtue, as well as privilege; but freedom of the seas does not mean piracy, nor freedom of the land, brigandage; nor freedom of the senate, freedom to cudgel a dissident member; nor freedom of the press, freedom to calumniate and lie. So, if patriotism be a virtue indeed, it cannot mean an exclusive devotion to our country’s interests,—for that is only another form of devotion to personal interests, family interests, or provincial interests, all of which, if not driven past themselves, are vulgar and immoral objects. Let us put away the Little Peddlingtonism unworthy of a great nation, and too prevalent among us. If the man who does not look beyond this natural life is of a somewhat narrow order, what must be the man who does not look beyond his own frontier or his own sea?

I confess that I dream of the day when an English statesman shall arise with a heart too large for England; having courage in the face of his countrymen to assert of some suggested policy,—“This is good for your trade; this is necessary for your domination: but it will vex a people hard by; it will hurt a people farther off; it will profit nothing to the general humanity: therefore, away with it!—it is not for you or for me.” When a British minister dares speak so, and when a British public applauds him speaking, then shall the nation be glorious, and her praise, instead of exploding from within, from loud civic mouths, come to her from without, as all worthy praise must, from the alliances she has fostered and the populations she has saved.

And poets who write of the events of that time shall not need to justify themselves in prefaces for ever so little jarring of the national sentiment imputable to their rhymes.

Rome: _February 1860_.

### _ITALY AND THE WORLD._
